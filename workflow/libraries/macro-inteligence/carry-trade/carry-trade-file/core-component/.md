# 3. Core Components

## Introduction

Every carry trade consists of several fundamental components working together.

Understanding these components is essential because changes in any one of them can affect the profitability, sustainability, and risk of the entire carry trade.

Within GCFIOS, these components are monitored individually and collectively to determine the current state of the global carry regime.

---

## 1. Funding Currency

The funding currency is the currency that investors borrow.

An effective funding currency generally has:

• Low interest rates

• Stable funding markets

• Deep liquidity

• Reliable access to credit

• Low borrowing costs

The objective is to obtain capital as cheaply as possible.

Historically, examples of funding currencies have included:

• Japanese Yen (JPY)

• Swiss Franc (CHF)

However, funding currencies can change over time depending on global monetary policy and financial conditions.

---

## 2. Target Currency

The target currency is the currency into which borrowed funds are converted before investing.

Investors typically prefer target currencies that offer:

• Higher interest rates

• Stronger economic growth

• Attractive investment opportunities

• Stable financial systems

The target currency provides access to higher-return assets.

---

## 3. Interest Rate Differential

The interest rate differential is the difference between:

• The borrowing cost of the funding currency

and

• The return available in the target market.

This spread forms the economic foundation of the carry trade.

Generally:

Larger positive differentials increase the attractiveness of carry trades.

Smaller differentials reduce their attractiveness.

Negative differentials usually eliminate the incentive for traditional carry strategies.

---

## 4. Funding Cost

Funding cost represents the total cost of obtaining capital.

It may include:

• Central bank policy rates

• Money market borrowing costs

• Repo funding costs

• Credit spreads

• Financing costs charged by lenders

Lower funding costs improve potential carry profitability.

Higher funding costs reduce expected returns.

---

## 5. Investment Return

Investment return represents the expected reward from deploying borrowed capital.

Potential sources include:

• Interest income

• Government bond yields

• Corporate bond yields

• Dividend income

• Equity appreciation

• Commodity appreciation

• Capital gains

The objective is to generate returns that exceed total funding costs.

---

## 6. Exchange Rate Risk

Carry trades often involve borrowing one currency while investing in another.

As a result, changes in exchange rates can significantly affect profitability.

Even if the interest rate differential remains favourable, adverse currency movements may reduce or eliminate gains.

Exchange-rate risk is therefore one of the most important risks associated with carry trades.

---

## 7. Leverage

Many institutional carry strategies employ leverage.

Leverage increases:

• Potential returns

but also increases:

• Potential losses

Because leverage magnifies both profits and risks, changes in volatility or funding conditions can force rapid position reductions during periods of market stress.

---

## 8. Holding Period

Carry trades are generally medium- to long-term strategies.

The holding period depends on:

• Monetary policy stability

• Interest rate expectations

• Funding conditions

• Market volatility

• Risk sentiment

Carry positions may remain open for weeks, months, or even years if favourable conditions persist.

---

## 9. Liquidity

Liquidity determines how easily capital can be borrowed, deployed, and exited.

Healthy carry environments typically require:

• Deep funding markets

• Efficient currency markets

• Stable credit conditions

• Reliable market liquidity

Deteriorating liquidity often increases execution costs and can accelerate carry trade unwinds.

---

## 10. Volatility

Volatility directly affects carry trade risk.

Low and stable volatility generally supports carry expansion because investors are more willing to hold leveraged positions.

Rapid increases in volatility often reduce risk appetite and increase the probability of carry contraction or unwind.

---

## 11. Risk Appetite

Carry trades depend heavily on investor willingness to assume risk.

When confidence is high, investors are generally more willing to borrow cheaply and purchase higher-yielding assets.

When fear dominates financial markets, investors often reduce leverage and unwind carry positions.

Risk appetite therefore plays a central role in determining the strength of the global carry regime.

---

## Relationships Between Components

A successful carry trade requires these components to work together.

Funding Currency
↓

Low Funding Cost
↓

Positive Interest Rate Differential
↓

Attractive Investment Return
↓

Stable Exchange Rates
↓

Adequate Liquidity
↓

Controlled Volatility
↓

Healthy Risk Appetite
↓

Carry Trade Expansion

If one or more of these components deteriorate, the attractiveness of carry trades may decline, increasing the probability of contraction or unwind.

---

## GCFIOS Interpretation

Within GCFIOS, carry trades are never evaluated using a single variable.

Instead, the system assesses the interaction between funding costs, interest rate differentials, expected returns, exchange-rate risk, leverage, liquidity, volatility, and investor risk appetite.

This multi-factor approach provides a more comprehensive assessment of whether global carry conditions are strengthening, remaining stable, weakening, or transitioning toward an unwind.
