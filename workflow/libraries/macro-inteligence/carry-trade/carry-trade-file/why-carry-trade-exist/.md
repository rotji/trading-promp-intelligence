# 2. Why Carry Trades Exist

## Introduction

Carry trades exist because capital is not priced equally across the global financial system.

Every country has its own:

• Interest rates

• Inflation

• Monetary policy

• Economic growth

• Credit conditions

• Currency risk

• Investment opportunities

These differences create opportunities for investors to move capital from one market to another in search of higher returns.

---

## Fundamental Economic Principle

Capital naturally seeks the highest risk-adjusted return.

If two investment opportunities have similar levels of risk but one offers a higher expected return, investors have an incentive to allocate capital toward the higher-returning opportunity.

Carry trades are one of the mechanisms through which this reallocation occurs.

---

## Interest Rate Differentials

The most common reason carry trades exist is the difference in interest rates between countries.

Example:

Country A

Interest Rate:

0.25%

Country B

Interest Rate:

5.00%

An investor may borrow in Country A at a low funding cost and invest in assets located in Country B that offer a higher yield.

The objective is to earn the spread between the funding cost and the investment return.

---

## Differences in Monetary Policy

Central banks do not follow identical monetary policies.

Some maintain:

• Low interest rates

• Accommodative monetary policy

• Abundant liquidity

Others maintain:

• Higher interest rates

• Tight monetary policy

• Restrictive liquidity

These policy differences create persistent funding advantages that encourage cross-border capital flows.

---

## Global Capital Allocation

Large institutional investors continuously allocate capital across countries and asset classes.

Rather than leaving capital in low-return environments, they seek opportunities that improve portfolio returns while remaining within acceptable risk limits.

Carry trades therefore become part of the broader global capital allocation process.

---

## Funding Efficiency

Some currencies consistently provide lower funding costs than others.

These currencies become attractive sources of financing because borrowing costs remain relatively inexpensive.

Lower funding costs increase the potential profitability of investing elsewhere.

---

## Yield Enhancement

Carry trades are often used to improve portfolio income.

Potential sources of return include:

• Higher policy interest rates

• Government bond yields

• Corporate bond yields

• Dividend-paying equities

• Income-producing investment strategies

By combining low-cost funding with higher-yielding assets, investors attempt to enhance total portfolio returns.

---

## Portfolio Diversification

Carry trades may also support portfolio diversification.

Institutional investors often combine carry strategies with other investment approaches to diversify sources of return rather than relying on a single market or asset class.

---

## Liquidity Transmission

Carry trades are not merely investment decisions.

They also transmit liquidity throughout the global financial system.

Borrowed capital leaves funding markets and enters investment markets, influencing:

• Foreign exchange

• Bonds

• Equities

• Credit

• Commodities

• Cryptocurrencies

As a result, carry trades become an important channel through which global liquidity is redistributed.

---

## Risk Compensation

Carry returns are not free profits.

Higher expected returns compensate investors for accepting risks such as:

• Exchange-rate movements

• Interest-rate changes

• Funding cost increases

• Volatility

• Liquidity deterioration

• Policy changes

• Geopolitical events

The existence of these risks explains why carry opportunities can persist over time.

---

## GCFIOS Interpretation

Within GCFIOS, carry trades exist because differences in monetary policy, funding costs, and expected returns encourage capital to move across borders.

These capital movements influence global liquidity, risk appetite, exchange rates, bond markets, equity markets, commodity markets, and other financial assets.

Carry trades are therefore analysed not simply as investment strategies, but as one of the primary mechanisms through which global capital is redistributed and macroeconomic pressures are transmitted across markets.
